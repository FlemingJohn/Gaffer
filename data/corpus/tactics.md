# Grassroots Tactical Knowledge Base

Each `##` section is one retrievable snippet (the ingest script splits on these
headings). Kept in plain coach language: 18 attacking tactics, then 12 defensive.

## Attacking — Overload a flank
Create a 2-v-1 or 3-v-2 down one wing by combining the full-back, winger and
near-side midfielder. Draw the defender to the ball, then release the free man
outside or inside him. If they double up to stop it, the switch to the far side
is on — so keep a player wide on the opposite flank.

## Attacking — Switch the point of attack
When the opposition shifts across to crowd one side, the space is on the far
side. Play a quick diagonal to the free winger before the defence can slide over.
The receiver must stay wide and high to stretch the pitch; if they drift inside,
the width — and the advantage — disappears.

## Attacking — Build through the thirds
Play out with purpose: centre-backs split, the holding midfielder drops or pivots,
full-backs push on. Move the ball to move the defence, then break a line with a
forward pass when a receiver is free between their units. Patience in the first
third earns the space in the final third.

## Attacking — Third-man runs
The passer plays to a first receiver, whose lay-off releases a third player
already on the move. It beats tight marking because the runner is tracked late.
Great for breaking a compact midfield: bounce it off the striker and run beyond.

## Attacking — Overlapping full-backs
The full-back sprints outside the winger to give width and a crossing option,
while the winger drives inside carrying a defender. It creates a 2-v-1 on the
touchline. Time the overlap as the winger receives, and make sure a midfielder
covers the space the full-back vacated.

## Attacking — Underlapping runs
Instead of going outside, the full-back or midfielder runs inside the winger,
into the channel between full-back and centre-back. It suits a winger who likes
to stay wide and cross, and it attacks the half-space where defenders are unsure
who picks up.

## Attacking — Play in behind a high line
If their defence pushes high, the grass is behind them. Hit early balls over or
through for a striker timing a run onto the last shoulder. Avoid slow sideways
build-up that lets them stay set — one well-timed pass beats ten square ones.

## Attacking — Counter-attack in transition
The moment you win the ball, look forward first. Break with one or two quick
passes into the space their attack left behind, before they recover shape. Runners
beyond the ball are the priority; the safe sideways pass kills the counter.

## Attacking — Wing play and crossing
Get the ball wide and early, commit the full-back, and deliver. Vary the cross:
whipped to the near post, cut-back to the edge, or floated to the back post.
Attack the box with three — near post, penalty spot, back post — plus one holding
at the top for the cut-out.

## Attacking — Cut-backs from the byline
Getting to the byline and pulling it back is one of the highest-value chances in
grassroots football, because it drags defenders towards their own goal facing the
wrong way. Aim for a runner arriving at the penalty spot and the edge of the area.

## Attacking — Central combinations (one-twos)
In tight central areas, give-and-go passing beats dribbling. Play a one-two around
a defender: pass, sprint past, receive the return in behind. Two quick combinations
can cut through a packed midfield that a single pass cannot.

## Attacking — Drop the striker to create space
Have the striker drop into midfield (a false-nine idea). Either a centre-back
follows — opening space in behind for a midfielder or winger to run into — or they
don't, and the striker gets time on the ball between the lines. Either way you win.

## Attacking — Corners
Assign clear roles: near-post flick-on, back-post target, penalty-spot arrival,
and one player on the edge for the second ball. Mix an out-swinger to attack with
an in-swinger that threatens the keeper. Rehearse one short-corner routine to
unbalance a zonal defence.

## Attacking — Attacking free kicks
Wide free kicks are crossing opportunities — treat them like a set corner with
timed runs. Central ones near the box: decide shot or delivery early. A simple
disguised routine (roll it sideways for a runner, or a dummy over the ball) beats
a hopeful strike into the wall.

## Attacking — Long throw-ins
A long throw into the box is effectively a free set piece. Flick it on at the near
post for runners behind, and load the six-yard box. It's a cheap, repeatable
weapon for a team with one big thrower, and it forces nervy clearances.

## Attacking — Counter-press to win it back high
The instant you lose the ball, the nearest two or three players press immediately
to win it back within seconds, while the opposition is still unbalanced from
defending. If it's not won quickly, drop into shape. Best when your forwards are
compact and close to the ball at the moment of loss.

## Attacking — Attack the half-spaces
The half-spaces (between the full-back and centre-back channels) are where you
create without going fully wide. Slide a midfielder or inside forward into that
pocket, facing forward, and you can slip runners in behind or shoot across goal.

## Attacking — Quick restarts
Catch them before they set: take throw-ins, free kicks and goal kicks fast when
players are out of position. A quick throw down the line or a fast free kick into
space turns a stoppage into a chance. Have the nearest player take it, not the
"designated" one.

## Defending — Set up a low block
Under sustained pressure or protecting a lead, drop into two banks of four about
25 yards from goal. Stay compact between the lines, deny the centre, and force
play wide. Forwards defend by screening passing lanes, not chasing. Break out on
the counter with one or two quick passes.

## Defending — Mid block
Defend from around the halfway line: invite them to build, then press once the
ball goes wide or a heavy touch invites pressure. It balances protecting space
with winning the ball higher than a low block, without the risk of a full high
press.

## Defending — High line and offside trap
Squeeze up to compress the pitch and hold attackers offside, stepping together on
a cue (usually the moment the passer's head goes down). It needs a quick, brave
back line and a keeper who sweeps. The danger is the ball in behind — one player
out of line breaks the trap.

## Defending — Stay compact between the lines
The distance between your defence and midfield is where good teams hurt you. Keep
those two banks within 10–15 yards so there's no pocket for their playmaker.
Shuffle across as a unit towards the ball; the far-side players tuck in rather
than holding width.

## Defending — Delay and jockey (1-v-1)
When isolated against an attacker, don't dive in. Approach under control, get
side-on, show them away from goal (usually towards the touchline or their weaker
foot), and buy time for support to arrive. The tackle comes only when the touch
is heavy or help is there.

## Defending — Double up on the winger
When a dangerous winger keeps beating your full-back, send the near-side
midfielder or winger across to make it 2-v-1. Screen the inside pass and force
them down the line into a dead end. The trade-off is the switch, so the rest of
the unit must slide over.

## Defending — Defend crosses
Set the back line to defend the space, not ball-watch. Attack the ball at its
highest point, defend the near post first, and mark the most dangerous runners
man-to-man with zonal cover on the posts. The holding midfielder guards the top of
the box for cut-backs and second balls.

## Defending — Defend corners
Concession from corners is a marking or gap problem, not bad luck. Assign each
defender a job — man, zone, or near/far post — and confirm it before the match. If
you concede, find who lost their man and re-confirm assignments rather than
reshuffling everyone. Keep one on each post if you defend zonally.

## Defending — Win second balls
Second balls are the loose balls after a tackle, header or clearance. Losing them
means you're outnumbered around the contact point. Push a midfielder higher to
support first contact and tell players to anticipate the drop rather than
ball-watch. It's positioning before the duel, not athleticism during it.

## Defending — Pressing triggers
A press must be coordinated, not individual. Common triggers: a backward pass, a
heavy first touch, or a pass to a full-back with no forward option. On the
trigger, the nearest player presses the ball and everyone else shifts to cut
passing lanes. Pressing without a trigger just opens gaps behind the presser.

## Defending — Track runners from midfield
Late runners from midfield cause more goals at grassroots than strikers do.
Midfielders must recognise and track their runner all the way, communicating
hand-offs at the edge of the box. Don't get ball-watching drawn to the ball while
your man runs off the back of you.

## Defending — Managing the game state
Defending with a lead late on: drop the line a touch, keep two banks compact,
force play wide, and don't chase. Use the ball to rest — keep possession in safe
areas and slow restarts. A fresh, disciplined substitute in a high-energy role
buys the most when legs are tiring.
