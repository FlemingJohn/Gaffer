# Grassroots Tactical Knowledge Base

Each `##` section below is one retrievable snippet. The ingest script splits on
these headings, so keep each section self-contained and in plain coach language.

## Wide overload on one flank
When the opposition repeatedly creates a 3-v-2 or 2-v-1 down one wing, your
full-back and winger on that side are being pulled apart. Counter by having the
near-side central midfielder shuttle across to make it 3-v-3, or drop the winger
to double up on their full-back. The trade-off is you concede the switch of play
to the far side, so the opposite winger must tuck in and be ready to sprint back.

## Defending a high opposition line
If the other team pushes their defence high, the space is in behind. Play a quick
forward early — a striker peeling onto the last shoulder, timed runs from
midfield. Avoid slow build-up that lets them stay compact. One direct ball beats
ten sideways passes here.

## Our defensive line caught too high
When your own back line steps up but the midfield does not press the ball, the
opposition's forward gets time to play the ball in behind. Fix: either drop the
back line 5–10 yards and defend the space, or push the nearest midfielder to
pressure the passer so the through-ball is never clean. Pick one — half-measures
get punished.

## Losing second balls in midfield
Second balls are the loose balls after a tackle, header, or clearance. If you
keep losing them, you are outnumbered around the contact point. Push a midfielder
higher to support the first contact, and tell players to anticipate the drop
rather than ball-watch. Winning second balls is about positioning before the
duel, not athleticism during it.

## Pressing triggers
A press should be coordinated, not individual. Common triggers: a backward pass,
a heavy first touch, a pass to a full-back with no easy forward option. On the
trigger, the nearest player presses the ball and everyone else shifts to cut
passing lanes. Pressing without a trigger just opens gaps behind the presser.

## Setting up a low block
When you are under sustained pressure and want to protect a lead, drop into two
banks of four roughly 25 yards from your own goal. Stay compact between the lines,
deny central penetration, and force play wide. Forwards defend by screening, not
chasing. Break out on the counter with one or two quick passes.

## Switching the point of attack
If the opposition is compact on one side, the space is on the far side. Encourage
a quick switch — a diagonal to the free winger — before the defence can shift
across. The key is the receiving winger staying wide and high to stretch the
pitch; if they drift inside there is no width to exploit.

## Managing a tiring team late in the game
Fatigue shows as a dropping line and slower pressing. Simplify: shorten passing
distances, keep the ball to rest, and make defensive shape the priority over
pushing numbers forward. A fresh substitute in a high-energy role (pressing
forward or shuttling midfielder) buys the most.

## Restart discipline on set pieces
Concession from set pieces is usually a marking or zonal-gap problem, not bad
luck. Assign each defender a job — man, zone, or near/far post — and confirm it
before the match. At half-time, if you conceded from a corner, name who lost
their man and re-confirm the assignments rather than reshuffling everyone.
